This is an invitation to a walk. At every crossroads, we will ask the same question: **what if we wanted to reverse that?** It is this question, asked again and again, that builds the entire architecture of numbers from the ground up.

## The Natural Numbers

<StepQuestion question="What if something exists?" />

We have to begin somewhere. Mathematics begins with an idea of disarming simplicity: that _something exists_. That something is `1`.

<TheOne />

Now we attach a rule of motion:
> **If a number exists, its successor ‚Äî that number plus one ‚Äî exists too.**

<TheSuccessor />

Apply it once: $1 + 1 = 2$. Again: $2 + 1 = 3$. Then $4$, $5$, $6$ ‚Äî the mechanism is as simple as it is inexhaustible.

<TheNaturals />

<StepConsequence>
  From one brick and one generative rule, an infinite set emerges. <strong>Multiplication</strong> was never a new rule ‚Äî it is compact notation for repeated addition, already contained in the successor from the beginning.
</StepConsequence>

## Zero and the Negative Numbers

<StepQuestion question="Can we undo an addition?" />

If we can add, we ask: _can we reverse it?_ Walking $3$ steps forward and returning to where we started ‚Äî what operation does that?

We invent subtraction. For a while it is comfortable: $5 - 2 = 3$, $7 - 4 = 3$, all results land safely in $\mathbb{N}$.

Then someone dares to write $3 - 3$. Silence. The result _should_ exist ‚Äî you advance $3$, retreat $3$, you are back at the origin ‚Äî but $\mathbb{N}$ has **no symbol for nothing**.

And $3 - 5$ is worse: the answer lives nowhere in our universe.

<TheZero />

The universe is **not closed** under subtraction. We have one option: enlarge it. Not by choice ‚Äî by logical necessity. We invent:

1. **Zero**, $0$ ‚Äî the solution to $n - n = 0$. The neutral element, the return to nothing.
2. **Negative numbers** ‚Äî so $3 - 5 = -2$ has somewhere to live.

<TheIntegers />

<StepConsequence>
  The number line undergoes a founding symmetry break. It no longer has a beginning. It stretches infinitely in both directions, with zero as the centre around which every positive has its perfect negative mirror. The integers $\mathbb{Z}$ are born ‚Äî closed under subtraction at last.
</StepConsequence>

<StepDivider step="03" symbol="‚Ñö" />

## The Rational Numbers

<StepQuestion question="Can we undo a multiplication?" />

The same reasoning repeats. If we can multiply, can we _undo_ a multiplication?

We invent division. Again, things go well: $6 \div 2 = 3$, $10 \div 5 = 2$.

But $1 \div 2$ has no integer answer ‚Äî no number in $\mathbb{Z}$ multiplied by $2$ gives $1$. The universe is non-closed again.

<TheDivision />

<TheRationals />

<StepConsequence>
  The number line becomes <strong>dense</strong>. Between any two integers there were nothing ‚Äî a void. Now there are infinitely many rationals: $\frac{3}{2}$, $\frac{5}{4}$, $\frac{99}{100}$‚Ä¶ and between any two of those, infinitely more. $\mathbb{Q}$ is born ‚Äî closed under division.
</StepConsequence>

> To feel this: between $\frac{1}{2}$ and $\frac{1}{3}$, find their average $\frac{5}{12}$. Between $\frac{1}{2}$ and $\frac{5}{12}$, find $\frac{11}{24}$. This game never ends.

Three steps in, it is worth pausing. Each time we asked _"what if we reversed that?"_ a new universe emerged ‚Äî strictly larger, strictly richer ‚Äî from the wreckage of the old one's limitations.

## Taming the Explosion ‚Äî Logarithms

<StepQuestion question="Given the result, what exponent produced it?" />

Iterating multiplication leads to exponentiation: $a^n$ means multiplying $a$ by itself $n$ times. Growth becomes brutally fast.

| $n$ | $10^n$            |
| --- | ----------------- |
| 1   | 10                |
| 3   | 1,000             |
| 6   | 1,000,000         |
| 9   | 1,000,000,000     |
| 12  | 1,000,000,000,000 |

<Exponentiation />

Now we ask the reverse: _given a result, what exponent produced it?_ Finding $x$ such that $10^x = 1000$ is computing the **logarithm** base $10$ of $1000$.

$$\log_{10}(1000) = 3 \qquad \text{because} \qquad 10^3 = 1000$$

<Logarithm />

<StepConsequence>
  Logarithms <strong>convert multiplication into addition</strong>:
  $$\log(A \times B) = \log(A) + \log(B)$$
  Exponential growth ‚Äî vertiginous, uncontrollable ‚Äî transforms into a slow, readable linear progression. This is why seismographs, decibels, and stellar magnitudes all use logarithmic scales.
</StepConsequence>

## The Wall of the Impossible

<StepQuestion question="What number, squared, gives $-1$?" />

We now have the real numbers $\mathbb{R}$ ‚Äî filling the gaps between rationals with $\sqrt{2}$, $\pi$, $e$. On this line, one rule seems inviolable:

> **Any real number, squared, is non-negative.**

Positive times positive is positive. Negative times negative is positive. This is the law of the real line.

And yet, here is an equation of disarming innocence:

$$x^2 + 1 = 0$$

It asks: _which number, squared, gives $-1$?_ By the law we just stated, no real number can answer.

<TheTransgression />

Faced with this impossibility, the Renaissance mathematicians ‚Äî Cardano, Bombelli ‚Äî could have filed this under "meaningless". They did not.

They **postulated the existence** of a number that did not exist. They named it $i$, for "imaginary", with one defining property:

$$\boxed{i^2 = -1}$$

<TheImaginaryUnit />

<StepConsequence>
  This is an act of pure logical will. You do not <em>prove</em> that $i$ exists ‚Äî you <em>decide</em> that it will, and accept all consequences. It is one of the most creative gestures in the history of mathematics.
</StepConsequence>

<StepDivider step="06" symbol="‚ÑÇ" />

## The Complex Plane ‚Äî A Revolution in Two Dimensions

<StepQuestion question="Where does $i$ go?" />

Now that $i$ exists, it needs a home. On the real line, every position is taken. $i$ cannot fit anywhere.

There is only one solution: **leave the line entirely**. We erect a second axis, perpendicular to the first ‚Äî the imaginary axis. The numerical universe does not extend: it _opens into a new dimension_.

Every complex number $Z$ is now a spatial coordinate:

$$Z = a + ib \qquad a,\ b \in \mathbb{R}$$

<TheComplexPlane />

<StepConsequence>
  In the complex plane, <strong>multiplication becomes rotation</strong>. Multiplying by $i$ does not stretch ‚Äî it rotates by $90^\circ$.
</StepConsequence>

Start from $1$ and multiply repeatedly by $i$:

- $1 \times i = i$ ‚Äî 90¬∞ rotation, we arrive at the top
- $i \times i = i^2 = -1$ ‚Äî another 90¬∞, we are on the left
- $-1 \times i = -i$ ‚Äî another 90¬∞, we are at the bottom
- $-i \times i = 1$ ‚Äî final 90¬∞, we are back where we started

<TheRotationCycle />

## Euler's Formula ‚Äî The Improbable Convergence

Our journey ends on an encounter nothing could have predicted. Five mathematical entities, born in radically different contexts and separated by centuries, converge into a single equation.

The introductions:

- **$0$** ‚Äî the additive identity, born from cancelling a displacement _(Step 02)_
- **$1$** ‚Äî the founding brick, our absolute starting point _(Step 01)_
- **$\pi$** ‚Äî the constant of the circle, appearing whenever curves and cycles are involved
- **$e$** ‚Äî the base of continuous growth, arising whenever something grows proportionally to itself
- **$i$** ‚Äî our imaginary unit, born from refusing to surrender to $x^2 = -1$ _(Step 05)_

By studying how $e^x$ behaves with an _imaginary_ input, Euler discovered a stunning relationship. When the exponent is $i\pi$, the infinite series defining $e^x$, $\cos(x)$, and $\sin(x)$ all collapse to the same point:

<MathBox label="Euler's Identity">
  e<sup class="text-2xl">iœÄ</sup> + 1 = 0
</MathBox>

<StepConsequence>
  Five constants that seemed to belong to entirely separate universes ‚Äî algebra, circular geometry, natural growth, imaginary algebra ‚Äî <strong>are deeply one</strong>.
</StepConsequence>

Take a step back and see the full path. We began with the brick `1`, equipped with one rule: the successor. That rule, pushed by the simple question _"what if we reversed it?"_, generated the integers ‚Äî then the negatives ‚Äî then fractions ‚Äî then had to domesticate unmanageable scales by inventing logarithms ‚Äî then finally shattered its own dimension to make room for $i$.

And at the end of this journey, all those worlds are woven together in a five-symbol formula.

<TheMasterPiece />

> Complexity was not _imposed_ on mathematics from outside. It _emerged_ from within, driven solely by the questions we dared to ask.

Each arrow is a question. Each set is an answer. And the walk ‚Äî it never quite ends.
