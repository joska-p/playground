This is an invitation to a walk. At every crossroads, we will ask the same question: **what if we wanted to reverse that?** It is this question, asked again and again, that builds the entire architecture of numbers from the ground up.

<Hero />

<StepHeader step="01" symbol="ℕ" />

## The Natural Numbers

<Callout variant="question" question="What if something exists?" />

We have to begin somewhere. Mathematics begins with an idea of disarming simplicity: that _something exists_. That something is $1$.

<TheOne />

Now we attach a rule of motion:
> **If a number exists, its successor — that number plus one — exists too.**

<TheSuccessor />

Apply it once: $1 + 1 = 2$. Again: $2 + 1 = 3$. Then $4$, $5$, $6$ — the mechanism is as simple as it is inexhaustible.

<Callout variant="consequence">
  From one brick and one generative rule, an infinite set emerges. **Multiplication** was never a new rule — it is compact notation for repeated addition, already contained in the successor from the beginning.
</Callout>

<TheNaturals />

<StepHeader step="02" symbol="ℤ" />

## Zero and the Negative Numbers

<Callout variant="question" question="Can we undo an addition?" />

If we can add, we ask: _can we reverse it?_ Walking $3$ steps forward and returning to where we started — what operation does that?

We invent subtraction. For a while it is comfortable: $5 - 2 = 3$, $7 - 4 = 3$, all results land safely in $\mathbb{N}$.

Then someone dares to write $3 - 3$. Silence. The result _should_ exist — you advance $3$, retreat $3$, you are back at the origin — but $\mathbb{N}$ has **no symbol for nothing**.

And $3 - 5$ is worse: the answer lives nowhere in our universe.

<TheZero />

The universe is **not closed** under subtraction. We have one option: enlarge it. Not by choice — by logical necessity. We invent:

1. **Zero**, $0$ — the solution to $n - n = 0$. The neutral element, the return to nothing.
2. **Negative numbers** — so $3 - 5 = -2$ has somewhere to live.

<Callout variant="consequence">
  The number line undergoes a founding symmetry break. It no longer has a beginning. It stretches infinitely in both directions, with zero as the centre around which every positive has its perfect negative mirror. The integers $\mathbb{Z}$ are born — closed under subtraction at last.
</Callout>

<TheIntegers />

<StepHeader step="03" symbol="ℚ" />

## The Rational Numbers

<Callout variant="question" question="Can we undo a multiplication?" />

The same reasoning repeats. If we can multiply, can we _undo_ a multiplication?

We invent division. Again, things go well: $6 \div 2 = 3$, $10 \div 5 = 2$.

But $1 \div 2$ has no integer answer — no number in $\mathbb{Z}$ multiplied by $2$ gives $1$. The universe is non-closed again.

<TheDivision />

<Callout variant="consequence">
  The number line becomes **dense**. Between any two integers there were nothing — a void. Now there are infinitely many rationals: $\frac{3}{2}$, $\frac{5}{4}$, $\frac{99}{100}$… and between any two of those, infinitely more. $\mathbb{Q}$ is born — closed under division.
</Callout>

<TheRationals />

> To feel this: between $\frac{1}{2}$ and $\frac{1}{3}$, find their average $\frac{5}{12}$. Between $\frac{1}{2}$ and $\frac{5}{12}$, find $\frac{11}{24}$. This game never ends.

Three steps in, it is worth pausing. Each time we asked _"what if we reversed that?"_ a new universe emerged — strictly larger, strictly richer — from the wreckage of the old one's limitations.

<StepHeader step="04" symbol="log" />

## Taming the Explosion — Logarithms

<Callout variant="question" question="Given the result, what exponent produced it?" />

Iterating multiplication leads to exponentiation: $a^n$ means multiplying $a$ by itself $n$ times. Growth becomes brutally fast.

| $n$ | $10^n$            |
| --- | ----------------- |
| 1   | 10                |
| 3   | 1,000             |
| 6   | 1,000,000         |
| 9   | 1,000,000,000     |
| 12  | 1,000,000,000,000 |

<TheExponent />

Now we ask the reverse: _given a result, what exponent produced it?_ Finding $x$ such that $10^x = 1000$ is computing the **logarithm** base $10$ of $1000$.

$$\log_{10}(1000) = 3 \qquad \text{because} \qquad 10^3 = 1000$$

<TheLogarithm />

<Callout variant="consequence">
  Logarithms **convert multiplication into addition**:
  $$\log(A \times B) = \log(A) + \log(B)$$
  Exponential growth — vertiginous, uncontrollable — transforms into a slow, readable linear progression. This is why seismographs, decibels, and stellar magnitudes all use logarithmic scales.
</Callout>

## The Wall of the Impossible

<Callout variant="question" question="What number, squared, gives $-1$?" />

We now have the real numbers $\mathbb{R}$ — filling the gaps between rationals with $\sqrt{2}$, $\pi$, $e$. On this line, one rule seems inviolable:

> **Any real number, squared, is non-negative.**

Positive times positive is positive. Negative times negative is positive. This is the law of the real line.

And yet, here is an equation of disarming innocence:

$$x^2 + 1 = 0$$

It asks: _which number, squared, gives $-1$?_ By the law we just stated, no real number can answer.

<TheTransgression />

Faced with this impossibility, the Renaissance mathematicians — Cardano, Bombelli — could have filed this under "meaningless". They did not.

They **postulated the existence** of a number that did not exist. They named it $i$, for "imaginary", with one defining property:

$$\boxed{i^2 = -1}$$

<Callout variant="consequence">
  This is an act of pure logical will. You do not _prove_ that $i$ exists — you _decide_ that it will, and accept all consequences. It is one of the most creative gestures in the history of mathematics.
</Callout>

<TheImaginaryUnit />

<StepHeader step="06" symbol="ℂ" />

## The Complex Plane — A Revolution in Two Dimensions

<Callout variant="question" question="Where does $i$ go?" />

Now that $i$ exists, it needs a home. On the real line, every position is taken. $i$ cannot fit anywhere.

There is only one solution: **leave the line entirely**. We erect a second axis, perpendicular to the first — the imaginary axis. The numerical universe does not extend: it _opens into a new dimension_.

Every complex number $Z$ is now a spatial coordinate:

$$Z = a + ib \qquad a,\ b \in \mathbb{R}$$

<TheComplexPlane />

<Callout variant="consequence">
  In the complex plane, **multiplication becomes rotation**. Multiplying by $i$ does not stretch — it rotates by $90^\circ$.
</Callout>

Start from $1$ and multiply repeatedly by $i$:

- $1 \times i = i$ — 90° rotation, we arrive at the top
- $i \times i = i^2 = -1$ — another 90°, we are on the left
- $-1 \times i = -i$ — another 90°, we are at the bottom
- $-i \times i = 1$ — final 90°, we are back where we started

<TheRotationCycle />

## Euler's Formula — The Improbable Convergence

Our journey ends on an encounter nothing could have predicted. Five mathematical entities, born in radically different contexts and separated by centuries, converge into a single equation.

The introductions:

- **$0$** — the additive identity, born from cancelling a displacement _(Step 02)_
- **$1$** — the founding brick, our absolute starting point _(Step 01)_
- **$\pi$** — the constant of the circle, appearing whenever curves and cycles are involved
- **$e$** — the base of continuous growth, arising whenever something grows proportionally to itself
- **$i$** — our imaginary unit, born from refusing to surrender to $x^2 = -1$ _(Step 05)_

By studying how $e^x$ behaves with an _imaginary_ input, Euler discovered a stunning relationship. When the exponent is $i\pi$, the infinite series defining $e^x$, $\cos(x)$, and $\sin(x)$ all collapse to the same point:

$$e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0$$

<Callout variant="consequence">
  Five constants that seemed to belong to entirely separate universes — algebra, circular geometry, natural growth, imaginary algebra — **are deeply one**.
</Callout>

<TheMasterPiece />

Take a step back and see the full path. We began with the brick $1$, equipped with one rule: the successor. That rule, pushed by the simple question _"what if we reversed it?"_, generated the integers — then the negatives — then fractions — then had to domesticate unmanageable scales by inventing logarithms — then finally shattered its own dimension to make room for $i$.

And at the end of this journey, all those worlds are woven together in a five-symbol formula.

> Complexity was not _imposed_ on mathematics from outside. It _emerged_ from within, driven solely by the questions we dared to ask.

Each arrow is a question. Each set is an answer. And the walk — it never quite ends.
