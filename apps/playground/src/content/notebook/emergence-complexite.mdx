## The Natural Numbers

### The simplest possible start

We have to begin somewhere. Mathematics begins with an idea of disarming simplicity: the idea that *something exists*. That something is called `1`.

At this point the universe is tiny. It contains a single object, a single notion: the unit. But we immediately attach a rule of motion, a generative rule:

> **If a number exists, its successor — that number plus one — exists too.**

That is all. One rule. And yet it is enough to trigger an explosion.

### What one rule unleashes

Apply the rule once and you get $1 + 1 = 2$. Apply it again: $2 + 1 = 3$. Then $4$, $5$, $6$ — the mechanism is as simple as it is inexhaustible. Nothing stops it. It produces, mechanically and inevitably, the infinite sequence of natural numbers:

$$\mathbb{N} = \{1,\ 2,\ 3,\ 4,\ 5,\ \ldots\}$$

Notice what just happened: from **one brick** and **one move**, we have constructed an infinite set. This is our first glimpse of *emergence* — the property a simple system has of generating richness wildly disproportionate to its starting rules.

**Multiplication** isn't even a new rule: it is just compact notation for repeated addition. $3 \times 4$ means "add $4$ to itself $3$ times". It is entirely contained within addition, already there from the beginning.

## Zero and the Negative Numbers

### The question of reversal

We know how to add. Naturally, a question forms: *can we undo what we just did?* If I walk $3$ steps forward and want to return to where I started, what operation should I perform?

We invent subtraction. For a while, everything is fine: $5 - 2 = 3$, $7 - 4 = 3$, all results land safely inside $\mathbb{N}$. Home is comfortable.

Then one day, someone dares to write $3 - 3$. Silence. The result *should* exist — you advance $3$, retreat $3$, you are back at the origin — but there is no symbol in $\mathbb{N}$ for "no displacement at all". And what about $3 - 5$? That is even worse: the answer lives nowhere in our universe.

### Forcing the expansion

Our universe is **not closed** under subtraction. To fix this, we have exactly one option: enlarge it. Not by choice — by logical necessity. We are compelled to invent:

1. **Zero**, $0$ — the unique solution to $n - n = 0$. The neutral element, the origin, the return to nothing.
2. **Negative numbers** — so that $3 - 5 = -2$ has somewhere to live. A number that "is missing", a debt, a displacement reversed.

The integers $\mathbb{Z}$ are born:

$$\mathbb{Z} = \{\ldots,\ -3,\ -2,\ -1,\ 0,\ 1,\ 2,\ 3,\ \ldots\}$$

In doing this, our number line undergoes a **founding symmetry break**. It no longer has a beginning. It stretches infinitely in both directions, with zero as the centre of gravity around which every positive number has its perfect negative mirror.

## The Rational Numbers

### The same pattern, again

You may have guessed it: the exact same reasoning is about to repeat, this time with multiplication. If we can multiply, can we *undo* a multiplication?

We invent division. Again, things go well for a while: $6 \div 2 = 3$, $10 \div 5 = 2$. But what happens with $1 \div 2$? There is no integer in $\mathbb{Z}$ that, when multiplied by $2$, gives $1$.

Our universe is non-closed again. We are forced to enlarge again.

### The number line becomes dense

We conceive the **rational numbers** $\mathbb{Q}$, written as fractions $\frac{a}{b}$ where $b \neq 0$:

$$\mathbb{Q} = \left\{\frac{a}{b}\ \middle|\ a \in \mathbb{Z},\ b \in \mathbb{Z}^*\right\}$$

What changes here is subtle but vertiginous. Before, between two consecutive integers like $1$ and $2$ there was *nothing* — a void. Now, between those same two points, there exist **infinitely many** rationals: $\frac{3}{2}$, $\frac{5}{4}$, $\frac{7}{5}$, $\frac{99}{100}$... And between any two of those rationals, however close, there are infinitely many more. The number line has become **dense**.

> To feel this: between $\frac{1}{2}$ and $\frac{1}{3}$, you can always find their average, $\frac{5}{12}$. Between $\frac{1}{2}$ and $\frac{5}{12}$, there is $\frac{11}{24}$. This game never ends.

Three steps in, it is worth pausing to see what we have built. Each time we asked *"what if we reversed that?"*, a new universe emerged — strictly larger, strictly richer — from the wreckage of the old one's limitations. The diagram below makes this nesting visible.

## Taming the Explosion — Logarithms

### When growth outruns us

Iterating multiplication leads naturally to exponentiation: $a^n$ means multiplying $a$ by itself $n$ times. Growth then becomes brutally fast.

| $n$ | $10^n$ |
|-----|--------|
| 1 | 10 |
| 3 | 1,000 |
| 6 | 1,000,000 |
| 9 | 1,000,000,000 |
| 12 | 1,000,000,000,000 |

In twelve steps we go from $10$ to a trillion. Astronomical distances, infinitesimal probabilities, the orders of magnitude of the physical universe — all of these grow at this speed, or faster.

### The inverse question: find the exponent

We ask the reverse: *given a result, what exponent produced it?* Finding $x$ such that $10^x = 1000$ is computing the **logarithm** base $10$ of $1000$. The answer is $3$.

$$\log_{10}(1000) = 3 \qquad \text{because} \qquad 10^3 = 1000$$

What makes logarithms so powerful is their fundamental property: they **convert multiplication into addition**.

$$\log(A \times B) = \log(A) + \log(B)$$

Multiplying two enormous numbers becomes adding two manageable ones. Exponential growth — vertiginous, uncontrollable — transforms into a slow, readable linear progression. This is precisely why seismographs, decibels, and stellar magnitudes all use logarithmic scales.

## The Wall of the Impossible

### An intimate enemy: the square

We now have the real numbers $\mathbb{R}$ — which fill in the gaps left by rationals with numbers like $\sqrt{2}$ or $\pi$ that "fall between the cracks" of fractions. On this line, one rule seems absolutely inviolable:

> **Any real number, squared, is non-negative.**

The reason is intuitive: multiply a positive by itself ($3 \times 3 = 9$) or a negative by itself ($-3 \times -3 = 9$) — the result is always positive. This is a universal law of the real line.

And yet, here is an equation of disarming innocence:

$$x^2 + 1 = 0$$

It asks: *which number, squared, gives $-1$?* By the law we just stated, no real number can answer. On the real line, this equation has **no solution**.

### The founding act of transgression

Faced with this impossibility, the Renaissance mathematicians — Cardano, Bombelli — could have filed this equation under "meaningless" and moved on. They did not.

They did something far more audacious: they **postulated the existence** of a number that did not exist. They gave it a name, $i$, for "imaginary", and assigned it one single defining property:

$$\boxed{i^2 = -1}$$

This is an act of pure logical will. You do not *prove* that $i$ exists — you *decide* that it will, and accept all consequences. It is one of the most creative gestures in the entire history of mathematics.

## The Complex Plane — A Revolution in Two Dimensions

### Where does $i$ go?

Now that $i$ exists, it needs a home. On the real line, every position is already occupied. $i$ cannot fit anywhere.

There is only one solution: **leave the line entirely**. Break free from one dimension and claim a new freedom: height.

We erect a second axis, **perpendicular** to the first — the imaginary axis. The numerical universe does not simply extend: it *opens into a new dimension*. Every complex number $Z$ is now a spatial coordinate in a two-dimensional plane:

$$Z = a + ib \qquad a,\ b \in \mathbb{R}$$

where $a$ is the real component (horizontal position) and $b$ the imaginary component (vertical position).

### Multiplication becomes rotation

This is where the complex plane reveals its magnificent strangeness. In the complex plane, **multiplying by $i$ does not change a number's size — it rotates it by 90°**.

Start from $1$ (sitting on the positive real axis) and multiply repeatedly by $i$:

- $1 \times i = i$ → 90° rotation, we arrive at the top
- $i \times i = i^2 = -1$ → another 90°, we are on the left
- $-1 \times i = -i$ → another 90°, we are at the bottom
- $-i \times i = 1$ → final 90°, we are back where we started

Four multiplications by $i$. Four rotations of 90°. One full turn. **Multiplication is no longer a scaling operation — it is a geometric choreography.**

## Euler's Formula — The Improbable Convergence

### Five strangers who were never supposed to meet

Our journey ends on an encounter nothing could have predicted. Five mathematical entities, born in radically different contexts and separated by centuries of development, converge into a single equation.

The introductions:

- **$0$** — the additive identity, born from the need to cancel a displacement *(Step 02)*
- **$1$** — the founding brick, our absolute starting point *(Step 01)*
- **$\pi \approx 3.14159\ldots$** — the constant ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter. It appears whenever curves and cycles are involved.
- **$e \approx 2.71828\ldots$** — the base of continuous growth and decay. It arises naturally in any phenomenon that "grows proportionally to itself": compound interest, radioactive decay, biological populations.
- **$i$** — our imaginary unit, born from the refusal to surrender to the equation $x^2 = -1$ *(Step 05)*

### The formula

By studying how the exponential function $e^x$ behaves when given an *imaginary* input, Leonhard Euler discovered a stunning relationship. When the exponent is $i\pi$, the infinite series defining $e^x$, $\cos(x)$, and $\sin(x)$ all collapse to the same point:

This formula is often called the most beautiful in all of mathematics. Not by convention — but because it says something profound: five constants that seemed to belong to entirely separate universes (algebra, circular geometry, natural growth, imaginary algebra) **are deeply one**.

### The loop closes

Take a step back and see the full path. We began with the brick `1`, equipped with one rule: the successor. That rule, pushed by the simple question *"what if we reversed it?"*, generated the integers — then the negatives — then fractions — then had to domesticate unmanageable scales by inventing logarithms — then finally shattered its own dimension to make room for $i$.

And at the end of this journey, all those worlds are woven together in a five-symbol formula. The entire tower, from the first brick to the complex plane, is laid out below.
