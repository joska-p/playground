This is an invitation to a walk.

At every crossroads we will ask the same question: **what if we reversed this?** It is this question, asked again and again, that builds the whole architecture of numbers — from the ground up, step by step, each extension forced on us not by choice but by logical necessity.

<br />

<br />

## Step 01 — The Natural Numbers — $\mathbb{N}$

_What if something existed?_

We must begin somewhere. Mathematics starts from an idea of disarming simplicity: that _something exists_. That something is $1$.

Then we add a single rule of motion — **if a number exists, its successor also exists** — and we never have to stop.

```text
Apply it once 1 + 1 = 2
Again 2 + 1 = 3
Then 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9 ... toward infinity
```

The mechanism is as simple as it is tireless. From one brick and one generative rule, an infinite set unfolds.

Multiplication is not a new invention here. It is merely a compact notation for repeated addition — already contained in the successor from the very start.

## Step 02 — Zero and the Negative Numbers — $\mathbb{Z}$

_Can we undo an addition?_

If we can add, we ask: can we invert the operation? Walk forward 3 steps and return to your starting point. What operation does that describe?

We invent subtraction. For a while, everything holds:

```text
5 - 2 = 3
7 - 4 = 3
```

All results land safely in $\mathbb{N}$.

Then someone dares to write $3 - 3$.

Silence.

The result _should_ exist — we moved forward 3, we moved back 3, we returned to the origin. But $\mathbb{N}$ has no symbol for nothing. And $3 - 5$ is worse still: the answer lives nowhere in our universe. The universe is not **closed** under subtraction.

We have only one option: enlarge it. Not by choice — by logical necessity.

We invent **zero** as the solution to $n - n = 0$: the neutral element, the return to nothing. And we invent **negative numbers** so that $3 - 5 = -2$ has somewhere to live.

The number line undergoes a foundational symmetry break. It has no beginning anymore. It extends infinitely in both directions, with zero at the center — every positive balanced by its perfect negative mirror.

## Step 03 — The Rational Numbers — $\mathbb{Q}$

_Can we undo a multiplication?_

The same reasoning repeats. If we can multiply, can we invert the operation? We invent division.

Simple examples settle easily: $6 \div 2 = 3$, $10 \div 5 = 2$. Everything is fine as long as the result remains an integer.

But $1 \div 2$ has no integer answer. No integer, multiplied by 2, gives 1. The universe is not closed, once again.

We extend it. We allow numbers of the form $\dfrac{a}{b}$, where $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}$ and $b \neq 0$.

The number line becomes **dense**. Between any two integers, there is now an infinity of rationals — and between any two rationals, another infinity still.

To feel it: between $\tfrac{1}{2}$ and $\tfrac{1}{3}$, take the average $\tfrac{5}{12}$. Between $\tfrac{1}{2}$ and $\tfrac{5}{12}$, take $\tfrac{11}{24}$. The game never ends. There is no smallest gap, no place where the line runs out of names.

## Step 04 — Logarithms

_Given the result, what exponent produced it?_

Repeated multiplication becomes exponentiation: $a^n = \underbrace{a \times a \times \cdots \times a}_{n \text{ times}}$.

Growth can become staggering.

| $n$ |            $10^n$ |
| :-: | ----------------: |
|  1  |                10 |
|  3  |             1,000 |
|  6  |         1,000,000 |
|  9  |     1,000,000,000 |
| 12  | 1,000,000,000,000 |

We ask for the inverse: find $x$ such that $10^x = 1{,}000$.

This gives us the logarithm. The key insight is that multiplication collapses into addition under the log:

$$\log(A \times B) = \log(A) + \log(B)$$

Twelve orders of magnitude become a single digit. The logarithm is the instrument that makes the unmeasurable readable — the scale on which the universe itself is measured.

## Step 05 — The Imaginary Unit — $\mathbb{i}$

_What number, squared, gives $-1$?_

The reals $\mathbb{R}$ fill the line. Famous irrationals — $\sqrt{2}$, $\pi$, $e$ — all live there, filling the gaps the rationals left behind. The line now feels complete.

But it enforces one absolute law: **every real square is $\geq 0$**.

The equation $x^2 + 1 = 0$ has no real solution. None. The universe hits a wall — not a practical limitation, but a structural one built into the geometry of the line.

Mathematicians chose to act rather than retreat. They postulated the existence of a number $i$ such that:

$$i^2 = -1$$

Not a trick. Not a shorthand for something else. A legitimate new entity — admitted by the same logical necessity that once forced us to invent zero.

## Step 06 — The Complex Plane — $\mathbb{C}$

_Where does $i$ go?_

$i$ does not sit on the line. It cannot — the line has no room for it. So we build a second axis, perpendicular to the reals, and we let $i$ live on it.

{/* TODO: add a complex plane visualization */}

Every complex number takes the form:

$$Z = a + ib \qquad a,\, b \in \mathbb{R}$$

where $a$ is the **real part** and $b$ is the **imaginary part**. The line has become a plane.

And something unexpected reveals itself in this new geometry: multiplication in $\mathbb{C}$ is not just scaling — it is **rotation**. Multiplying by $i$ rotates by exactly $90°$. Multiplying by $i$ twice rotates by $180°$, which sends any number to its negative. Which is exactly what $i^2 = -1$ says — translated into geometry.

The algebra and the geometry were always saying the same thing.

## Epilogue — Euler's Formula

_Five symbols. Five different origins. One equation._

$$0 \qquad 1 \qquad \pi \qquad e \qquad i$$

$0$ — the void, born when subtraction outran the naturals.
$1$ — the origin, the first postulate, the brick.
$\pi$ — the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter, irrational and inevitable.
$e$ — the base of natural growth, the number that is its own derivative.
$i$ — the answer to the impossible square.

They come from different centuries, different problems, different branches of mathematics. They have no obvious reason to meet. And yet:

$$e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0$$

This is not a coincidence dressed up in symbols. It is a structural fact — a signature left by the internal logic of mathematics when you follow it far enough and refuse to stop at the walls.

<br />

> Each step shows the same pattern: pose an operation, notice a missing closure, extend the universe. The new universe always turns out to be richer, more symmetric, more geometric than the one before. The walk has no end.
