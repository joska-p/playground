This is an invitation to a walk.

At every crossroads we will ask a simple question:

> What if ?

It is this question, asked again and again, that builds the whole architecture of numbers.

From the ground up, step by step, each extension forced on us not by choice but by logic.

<br />

<br />

## Step 01 — The Natural Numbers — <span class="text-accent">$\mathbb{N}$</span>

We must begin somewhere.

Mathematics starts from a simple idea:

> What if something exists

> if a number exists, its successor also exists

<div class="not-prose text-secondary border-secondary/10 my-8 border-y py-8 text-center text-xl">
  $1 + 1 =$ something
</div>

## Step 02 — Zero and the Negatives — <span class="text-accent">$\mathbb{Z}$</ span>

> What if we undo the addition ?

- If we can add, can we reverse the operation ?

<br />

We invent subtraction. And for a while, everything holds:

All results land safely in <span class="text-accent">$\mathbb{N}$</span>.

Then someone dares to write

<div class="not-prose text-secondary border-secondary/10 my-8 border-y py-8 text-center text-xl">
  $1 - 1 = something$
</div>

> Silence.

...

> The result should exist.

<div class="not-prose text-secondary border-accent/20 text-accent/70 my-8 border-y py-8 text-center text-xl">
  $1 - 2$ is worse still
</div>

The answer lives nowhere in our universe <span class="text-accent">$\mathbb{N}$</ span>.

<br />

We have only one option: enlarge it.

> by logical necessity.

We invent a new symbol for the void <span class="text-secondary">$0$</span>, and a new direction for the deficit <span class="text-secondary">$-1$</span>.

<div class="not-prose text-secondary border-secondary/10 my-8 border-y py-8 text-center text-xl">
  $0 = n - n$ and $0 - n = -n$
</div>

And we give the **negative numbers** somewhere to live.

## Step 03 — The Rational Numbers — <span class="text-accent">$\mathbb{Q}$</span>

> What if we undo a multiplication ?

The same reasoning repeats. If we can multiply, can we invert the operation? We invent division.

And once again at the beginning, everything works. <span class="text-secondary">$4 \div2 = 2$</span>

> But what if.

<div class="not-prose text-secondary border-secondary/10 my-8 border-y py-8 text-center text-xl">
  $n = 1 \div2$
</div>

We invent new symbols.

<div class="not-prose text-secondary border-secondary/10 my-8 border-y py-8 text-center text-xl">
  $\dfrac{a}
  {b}$, where $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}$ and $b \neq 0$.
</div>

These new numbers need a home.

> The number line becomes dense.

## Step 04 — The Real Numbers — <span class="text-accent">$\mathbb{R}$</span>

From the multiplication we derive the exponentiation.

<div class="not-prose text-secondary border-secondary/10 my-8 border-y py-8 text-center text-xl">
  $$3 ^ 9 = 3 * 3 * 3 * 3 * 3 * 3 * 3 * 3 * 3$$
</div>

- It is just a fancy notation.

<br />

> What if we undo the exponentiation ?

- We need a new symbol: <span class="text-secondary">$\sqrt{}$</span>

<div class="not-prose text-secondary border-secondary/10 my-8 border-y py-8 text-center text-xl">
  $$\sqrt{a} = b$$ means that $$b^2 = a$$
</div>

<br />

For a while it was fine. Then someone asked.

> What if we undo the square of 2 ?

<div class="not-prose text-secondary border-secondary/10 my-8 border-y py-8 text-center text-xl">
  $n = \sqrt{2}$
</div>

<br />

The Real Numbers are born and they live in <span class="text-accent">$\mathbb{R}$</span>.

<div class="not-prose text-secondary border-secondary/10 my-8 border-y py-8 text-center text-xl">
  $\sqrt{2}$, $\pi$, $e$, $3.33333333...$, are real numbers
</div>

## Step 05 — The Imaginary Unit — <span class="text-accent">$\mathbb{I}$</span>

We have noticed that when you square a real number, the result is always positive.

- Even if you square a negative number, the result is still positive.

<div class="not-prose text-secondary border-secondary/10 my-8 border-y py-8 text-center text-xl">
  $(-2)^2 = 4$ or, $(-x)^2 = x^2$
</div>

It enforces one absolute law: **every real square is $\geq 0$**.

> What if we allow a square to be a negative number?

<div class="not-prose text-secondary border-secondary/10 my-8 border-y py-8 text-center text-xl">
  $x^2 = -1$
</div>

- The equation $x^2 + 1 = 0$ has no real solution.

Mathematicians chose to act rather than retreat. They postulated the existence of a number $i$ such that:

<div class="not-prose text-secondary border-secondary/10 my-8 border-y py-8 text-center text-xl">
  $$i^2 = -1$$
</div>

Not a trick. Not a shorthand for something else. A legitimate new entity — admitted by the same logical necessity that once forced us to invent zero.

## Step 06 — The Complex Plane — <span class="text-accent">$\mathbb{C}$</span>

> Where is <span class="text-secondary">$i$ ?</span>

- <span class="text-secondary">$i$</span> is not in the real world. It does not lie on the line.

We need to be creative.

We create a new dimension. A dimension with a solution for:

<div class="not-prose text-secondary border-secondary/10 my-8 border-y py-8 text-center text-xl">
  $i^2 = -1$.
</div>

Its axis is called the **imaginary axis**.

> We visualize it as a line perpendicular to the real axis.

And now with those two axis, the real numbers number line extends into a plane.

This is where the complex numbers live: <span class="text-accent">$\mathbb{C}$</span>

<ComplexPlane />

Every complex number takes the form:

<div class="not-prose text-secondary border-secondary/10 my-8 border-y py-8 text-center text-xl">
  $Z = a + ib \qquad a,\, b \in \mathbb{R}$
</div>

where <span class="text-secondary">$a$</span> is the **real part** and <span class="text-secondary">$b$</span> is the **imaginary part**. The line has become a plane.

A brand new world.

## Epilogue — Euler's Formula

> What if we bring them all together?

Five symbols. Five different origins. One equation.

<div class="not-prose text-secondary border-secondary/10 my-8 border-y py-8 text-center text-xl">
  $$0 \qquad 1 \qquad \pi \qquad e \qquad i$$
</div>

<br />

- **$0$** — the void, born when subtraction outran the naturals.
- **$1$** — the origin, the first postulate, the brick.
- **$\pi$** — the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter, irrational and inevitable.
- **$e$** — the base of natural growth, the number that is its own derivative.
- **$i$** — the answer to the impossible square.

They come from different centuries, different problems, and different branches of mathematics. They have no obvious reason to meet.

> And yet.

<figure title="Euler's Formula" class="text-center">
  <span class="text-accent text-5xl">$$e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0$$</span>
  <figcaption class="text-accent/80">Euler's Identity</figcaption>
</figure>

The walk has no end.
